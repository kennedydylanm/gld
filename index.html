Graduation with Leadership Distinction

Dylan Kennedy.

Chemical engineering student using technical work to build systems that are better for the environment. Pathway of Distinction: Professional and Civic Engagement.

“We can only see a short distance ahead, but we can see plenty there that needs to be done.” ~ Alan Turing

I'm a Chemical Engineering student in the Honors College at the University of South Carolina, graduating with Leadership Distinction through the Professional and Civic Engagement pathway. What drives me is pretty simple: I want to use engineering to leave things better than I found them. Chemical engineering is one of the most direct levers we have on the problems I care about, from capturing carbon to cutting the water and waste that industrial processes take for granted, and I came to Carolina wanting to actually work on those things rather than just study them.

What I've learned along the way is that a good environmental solution isn't worth much until someone decides to adopt it. A carbon-capture pitch, a plant-floor fix that saves water, a maintenance call in a residence hall: each one only mattered once I could explain why it was worth doing to the person who had to say yes. The three insights below trace that idea across my time here, and they close with how I plan to take one proven fix and push it further than a single facility. After graduation I want to build a career designing energy and process systems that are genuinely better for the environment, and getting the people around them to buy in.

What I Learned

Key Insights

Three moments where something I learned in a classroom and something I did in the real world shaped each other, and where I figured out that building a better environmental solution is only half the work. Getting people to adopt it is the other half.

01 A Good Environmental Solution Still Has to Be Understood to Matter
WTC · SPCH 140 Public Communication BTC · AIChE Annual Student Conference

Our Vitamin Cube team after the pitch at the AIChE Annual Student Conference, the ExxonMobil-sponsored ChemE Cube competition. This is the carbon-capture project that placed 2nd internationally.

SPCH 140 taught me something that sounded obvious until I had to do it under pressure: the same idea has to be reshaped, not just repeated, depending on who is listening. Audience analysis, figuring out what a specific group actually cares about and speaking to that, felt like a tidy communication concept at the time. It turned out to be the thing that decided whether my environmental work went anywhere.

It got real at the AIChE Annual Student Conference, where I represented two projects in the same week, both of them environmental, both needing completely different pitches. On the Vitamin Cube team I helped research a Solid Direct Air Capture additive, pyrolyzed mandarin peels treated with KOH that captured 6 wt.% CO2, and then pitched the process and its business case to a panel of judges acting as investors. For the ChemE Car project we had reverse engineered how a hydrogen fuel cell could power a car motor, and I presented that design to a panel of practicing engineers. The carbon-capture judges wanted to know if the idea could scale and pay for itself. The fuel-cell judges wanted to know if the engineering was sound. Same week, same person, two almost opposite conversations.

Preparing both at once forced me to actually use what SPCH 140 had only let me theorize. A carbon-capture additive and a hydrogen-powered motor are only as good as someone's willingness to fund or trust them, and that willingness lives entirely in how you frame the work. The Vitamin Cube pitch placed 2nd internationally, which told me the reframing held up under real scrutiny.

That is the lesson I keep coming back to. The climate problems I want to work on are full of technically sound ideas that never get adopted, and a big reason is that engineers are trained to prove things are correct, not to make people care about them. I've decided those are two different skills, and I want to be good at both, because a solution nobody buys into doesn't actually reduce any emissions.

02 Cutting Waste Only Counts if You Can Make the Case for It
WTC · ECHE-573 Next Energy BTC · Enviva Biomass Internship

On site at the WESP unit at Enviva Biomass's Greenwood, SC facility, where I developed and scoped the chemical additive discussed above.

ECHE-573 looks at the shift away from fossil fuels not as a purely technical puzzle but as one that has to survive economic, political, and social scrutiny all at once. A lot of the course was team based, and debating energy technologies with a group drove home a point I keep returning to: a technically correct decision isn't automatically the right one. It has to hold up when people weigh it through competing lenses, and cost is almost always one of them.

I ran into exactly that at my Enviva Biomass internship. I developed and scoped a chemical additive to eliminate Wet Electrostatic Precipitator (WESP) cleaning, working through Piping and Instrumentation Diagrams and real-time plant data, using Root Cause Analysis to find the problem, all inside Process Safety Management standards. The environmental win was real: the fix cuts the water and chemicals a plant otherwise burns through manually cleaning that equipment. But nobody was going to adopt it because it was greener. What moved it forward was framing it as roughly $150,000 a year in savings, with the reduced water and labor as part of the same story.

That's the version of sustainability I actually believe in. The environmental benefit and the business case aren't in tension, they're the same argument told two ways, and my job as an engineer is to build both at once. A fix that saves water and money gets funded. A fix that only saves water gets shelved.

It changed how I evaluate my own work. I now build the cost and environmental case right alongside the technical one, because that combination is what decides whether good engineering ever leaves the page and starts actually reducing waste.

03 Stewardship Starts With the Spaces People Live In
WTC · ENVR 321 Environmental Pollution & Health BTC · Resident Assistant

A "Ways to Bloom on Campus" board I built for my residents. Part of being an RA is looking after the place people live in, not just the policies they live under.

As a Resident Assistant I'm responsible for 28 residents, and part of that job is the physical condition of the place they live in. Some of the most recurring things I deal with are water related: leaks, moisture, the small building problems that are easy to treat as somebody else's department. For a long time that's exactly how I handled them. Report the leak, get maintenance involved, reassure the resident, move on. I was solving them procedurally without really thinking about why a little standing water mattered beyond the inconvenience.

ENVR 321 gave me the framework I'd been missing. The course is about how pollutants and stressors move through air, water, and soil and end up affecting whole communities, including how unaddressed moisture is the upstream cause of indoor air quality and mold problems in the places people live. That reframed the leaks I'd been brushing off as maintenance tickets. A leak isn't just a leak. It's the first step in a chain that can affect the health of everyone sharing that building, and catching it early is the cheapest, easiest place to intervene.

What changed wasn't the procedure, it was my understanding of the stakes. I'm not just closing out one resident's complaint, I'm keeping a small shared environment from turning into something that affects all 28 people in it. And that's the exact instinct that pulls me toward environmental engineering. The systems we build and maintain have downstream effects on the health of the people who depend on them, whether that's moisture in a residence hall or emissions from a plant. The scale is different but the responsibility is the same.

That's the part I didn't expect a housing job and an environmental health class to teach me together: stewardship isn't only something I'll practice someday as an engineer designing big systems. It starts with how I take care of the small, human-scale environment I'm already responsible for right now.

Applying What I've Learned

Leadership Plan

I already found one environmental fix that works. Here is how I plan to take it past the single plant where it lives now, because a solution that only runs in one place isn't finished yet.

The Issue

At Enviva's Greenwood facility, where I interned, cleaning the Wet Electrostatic Precipitator (WESP) eats up a real amount of water, labor, and chemicals, and for a long time everyone just treated that as a fixed cost of doing business. I developed and scoped a chemical additive that gets rid of WESP cleaning entirely, projected to save the facility over $150,000 a year while cutting the water and chemicals that cleaning burns through. The catch is that it only runs at one plant. Enviva operates several other facilities with comparable WESP systems, and the fix hasn't been evaluated, funded, or adopted anywhere else yet. I like this as a goal precisely because it's narrow and already proven. It isn't "make biomass sustainable," it's "take this one thing that works and get it into more plants," which is something I can actually do.

Recommendations / Solutions

The AIChE executive team I helped lead as Vice President. The sponsorship and fundraising we did is what funds the competition teams behind Insight #1.

My plan is to formally document the WESP additive as a standardized process change and build the case to pilot it at a second Enviva plant. That leans directly on Insight #2: a fix doesn't get adopted just because it's technically sound, it needs a financial and operational case wrapped around it, the same pairing of chemistry with P&ID review, real-time data, and Root Cause Analysis that got it approved the first time. It also leans on Insight #1, because the people I need to convince don't all care about the same thing. Operations will want to hear about downtime and labor. Sustainability leadership will want water reduction and emissions numbers. It's the same fix, told two ways, which is exactly the skill I practiced pitching Vitamin Cube to investors and the ChemE Car to engineers in one week.

I'll also lean on what I learned as Vice President of AIChE, where I brought in over $5,000 in funding and sponsorships for our competition teams. That job taught me what it actually takes to get an organization to say yes to a resource request: you build relationships with the people who hold the budget, and you show up with a clear, credible ask instead of just a good idea. Getting a second plant to try the WESP fix is the same kind of ask, just with more at stake.

Detailed Plan

StepHowWhy
1. Formalize the documentation Write up the WESP additive project as a standardized process change, including the P&ID review, chemistry, and cost/water-savings data already gathered. A one-off internship success has to become a repeatable, transferable process before anyone else can adopt it.
2. Identify a pilot facility Work with my Enviva supervisor to identify a second plant with a comparable WESP configuration. The fix needs to be validated somewhere other than the original site before it can be trusted as a general solution.
3. Build two versions of the pitch Create an operations-focused case (labor, downtime, safety) and a sustainability-focused case (water use, emissions) from the same underlying data. Different stakeholders approve budget for different reasons. Insight #1 in practice.
4. Secure pilot funding Present the case to plant leadership, using the stakeholder-relationship and ask-framing skills I built securing AIChE sponsorships. Even a proven fix needs someone to approve the budget and time to test it elsewhere.
5. Implement and monitor Work with the second plant's engineers to implement the additive and track cleaning frequency, chemical cost, and water use over a defined period (e.g., 3–6 months). Comparable, measured results are what would justify a company-wide rollout.

Evaluation

Success would be the second plant hitting a real share of the original site's savings. I'd set the bar at 70% of the original cost and water reduction to start, since things like water chemistry, equipment age, and throughput vary plant to plant and some drop-off is honest to expect. I'd lean on the pilot plant's own engineers and operators to flag anything that doesn't transfer cleanly, and I'd track the same numbers that made the first case land: cleaning frequency, chemical cost, and water use, measured before and after. What I really want to learn is whether this fix generalizes across Enviva's plants, and past that, how you take one engineering win and turn it into something a whole company does by default. That last skill, scaling a good idea into a standard, is the one I think matters most for actually moving the needle on sustainability, and it's the one I most want to keep practicing.
